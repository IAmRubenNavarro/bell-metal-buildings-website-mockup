Galvalume vs Galvanized: which substrate is right for your roof?

Published February 18, 2026 · 8 min read
By · Categories: Materials, DIY

Watch how to build a steel building from foundation to finish

A two-coat comparison most suppliers don't want you to know. Here's the practical take from Bell engineers and project managers — what we tell every buyer who asks, drawn from real builds shipped from our Aliquippa plant.

Step 1 — Get the foundation right

Your foundation is the only part of the build you cannot undo cheaply. Pour to your engineered anchor-bolt drawings (we ship those before steel ever leaves the mill). Double-check your dimensions before you call for inspection. Verify your anchor bolts project the right height above the slab — most kits assume 2.5 inches of stick-up, but always check the spec.

Common gotcha: contractors sometimes set anchor bolts to the wrong template. Take your stamped drawings to the pour day and verify before the truck leaves.

Step 2 — Stand the primary frame

Primary frame goes up in two phases: erect the columns, then drop in the rafters. For garage-sized buildings (up to about 40×60), two people with a scissor lift can manage. For larger spans you'll want a small crane on the day you set rafters.

Get the first end frame plumb and square. Every other frame in the building references off the first one. Use string lines and a laser level — eyeballing is what creates twisted buildings.

Step 3 — Add the secondary frame

Purlins (roof) and girts (walls) tie the primary frame together. They also give you something to bolt panels into. This is the slowest-feeling part of the build because there's a lot of bolting, but it's the easiest phase — the parts are light and the engineering is forgiving.

Step 4 — Install panels

Walls first, then roof. Each panel ships labeled to a specific bay. Start at one corner of the building and work outward. Use the stitch-screw pattern in your drawings — the spec exists because panel uplift in a windstorm depends on every screw being where the engineer drew it.

Pro tip: install on a day with low wind. Steel panels catch wind like sails. If you're working alone, never carry a panel above shoulder height in anything over a 10-mph breeze.

Step 5 — Trim, doors, and finish

Corner trim, ridge cap, eave trim, and rake trim go on after the panels. Walk doors and overhead doors typically install last so they don't get banged up during the panel phase.

Hang your gutters, install your windows, caulk the major seams, and you're done. Most owner-installed Bell garages get to this point in 2–4 weekends, depending on size and crew.
